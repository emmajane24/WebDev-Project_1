To: Acting Assistant Secretary of State for African Affairs - Troy D. Fitrell
From: Policy Advisor
Re: Countering Foreign Interference in Sudan to Advance U.S. Security and Strategic Interests
Date: 04/11/2025

Executive Summary

The ongoing civil conflict in Sudan presents a unique and serious challenge to US national interests and regional stability. Intelligence suggests that foreign interference has exacerbated this conflict, particularly that of entities operating out of the UAE and Russia. It is in the United States’ strategic interest to impose sanctions on the UAE, Russia, and affiliated entities for their destabilizing roles in the Sudanese Civil War and the human rights abuses committed by their proxies. Failure to act will allow these outside actors to deepen their influence and prevent a diplomatic end to the war.

Background

Sudan is entering its third year embroiled in a deep and devastating civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF). Stemming from an unsuccessful transition to civilian rule following the ousting of longtime President Omar al-Bashir in 2019, tensions gradually escalated into what effectively became a personal power struggle between the leaders of the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces over who would assume control of the country.

Currently, parts of the east and north are controlled by SAF, while most of the West—including the capital city Khartoum—is controlled by the RSF. Recently, the RSF presented a formal bid for legitimacy by signing a constitution proposing the creation of a breakaway state, while SAF forces have launched a new offensive, retaking strategic territory around Khartoum.

What started as internal tension and low-level conflict has escalated into full-scale warfare, creating a humanitarian crisis unlike many others. The conflict has resulted in the death of an estimated 150,000 individuals, the displacement of nearly 12.5 million people (both internally and externally), and created humanitarian needs for roughly 30 million people.

This volatile situation has left Sudan a fragile state on the brink of collapse, allowing powers like the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Russia, and Iran to expand their regional influence. Iran, in particular, has re-engaged with Sudan by supplying drones and military aid to the SAF—a strategic move consistent with Tehran's broader effort to project power across the Red Sea and challenge Western-aligned interests in the region. Given Iran’s long-standing opposition to U.S. strategic goals in the Middle East, its growing involvement in Sudan adds another layer of complexity to what is increasingly an undeclared proxy war.

Issue Analysis

The first option is for the US to impose targeted sanctions on foreign actors supporting the conflict, particularly those aiding the RSF, such as the UAE and Russia. This would be an ideal approach if the administration wishes to undermine their ability to take advantage of the instability of Sudan. This approach would be a building up of sanctions to freeze the transfer of assets and block any transactions related to weapons and gold that may be fueling and funding the RSF. However, it is important to consider the diplomatic consequences of these actions, as they may strain bilateral relationships with these actors.

The second option is for the US to increase intelligence sharing with Egypt, which has been a supporter of the SAF. This strategy would be ideal if the administration wishes to counter Iran's influence in this conflict and in turn the region. Increased aid to Egypt would give them increased ability to aid the SAF, making the group more reliant on a US-backed ally instead of a state with which the US has no formal diplomatic agreement. Additionally, because of Egypt's strategic interests in the region, help from the US may strengthen relations between the two countries. However, historically Iran has also had strategic interests in the region, and SAFs' increased reliance on Egypt may not deter the Iranian government from getting involved.

The third option is for the US to expand its Naval presence in the Red Sea, especially in and near the Strait of Bab el-Mandeb. This would be ideal if the administration desires to show strength without deep involvement. The mere presence of the US Navy would deter arms smuggling between countries, with arms shipments entering Sudan being the most important to monitor at this time. This move would disrupt the flow of resources that are fueling the RSF’s war effort. The main concern with this strategy would be the Houthis—who have a pretty large presence in the Red Sea—seeing this action as an act of aggression towards them.

Solutions and Strategic Recommendations

The best option moving forward is to impose targeted and rigorous sanctions on those outside groups—states and non-state—who are backing the RSF. This would include entities in the UAE and Russia, as well as any networks engaged in arms and gold smuggling or financial transfers. Because the RSF functions as a paramilitary group that is heavily reliant on the support of outside institutions, these kind of sanctions would disrupt their ability to operate and send a clear message that America will not tolerate the manipulation of Sudan, in its weakened state, by anyone for gain.

Additionally, it would allow the US to preserve its strength in the region without requiring American boots on the ground. Many analysts suggest that if the RSF were to stop receiving external support, the conflict would likely subside, as it is the RSF that engages in more escalation-related operations.

Reducing the RSF’s military capacity—without deploying U.S. troops—aligns with U.S. national security interests, as Sudan’s instability creates a power vacuum that could be exploited by anti-American groups. Sudan has previously been a hotbed of anti-American sentiment and propaganda, being listed as a state sponsor of terror for more than 25 years for groups such as al-Qaeda, even at the time of the September 11th attacks.

It is the RSF that has been primarily responsible for the human rights violations committed throughout this conflict, further making actions against them not only an issue of national security but a moral imperative. It is important to consider the relationship with Russia through this process, as they and the US have a more volatile history. Framing the sanctions as not being anti-Russia but as part of an established US commitment to curbing transnational criminal activity.

By leveraging tools such as the Global Magnitsky Act and CAATSA, the U.S. can act decisively without escalating broader geopolitical tensions. Additionally, sanctions on UAE-linked actors should be strategically targeted to minimize broader economic fallout, while diplomatic backchannels should be leveraged to reinforce shared interests.

US inaction will enable ongoing human rights violations to persist and likely intensify, while also paving the way for greater authoritarian influence in the region and further destabilization of the Red Sea region. The US cannot afford to take a neutral or passive stance in a conflict that increasingly poses concerns for US national interests.
